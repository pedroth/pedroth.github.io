My intuitive proof of green theorem


Let $F=[P(x,y),Q(x,y)]^T:\mathbb{R}^2\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^2$ be a function that describes a vector field and $g:[a,b]\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^2$ be a simple closed curve. Imagine that you have the following problem:

Measure how much the vector field $F$ is tangent to the curve $g$.

It seems easy to make such calculation. All you need to do is to compute the projection of the vector given by the field with the tangent vector of a curve in every point of the curve. Then add up all of those numbers. Simple right?

Well this kind of calculation is useful for a lot of different applications, like fluid dynamics, geometry , physics, etc.
Mathematicians formalize this question in a great way, in this article will formalize this question and develop a method to compute the answer. This method will be a sketch of the proof of Green's Theorem. Let's start :

The sum of the projections of a vector field with the tagent of a curve is given by : $$\oint_{a}^{b}F\cdot \hat{T}dl \; \; \; \;(1)$$
  Where  $\hat {T}$ is the unit tangent vector to curve $g$, and $dl = |\dot g(t)| dt$, which is a small arc length of the curve. So (1) becomes
$$\oint_{a}^{b}F(g(t))\cdot \frac{\dot g(t)}{|\dot g(t)|}|\dot g(t)| dt $$
$$\Leftrightarrow \oint_{a}^{b}F(g(t))\cdot \dot g(t) dt$$

Fig 1
It will be useful to define $p$ to $p:[a,b][c,d]\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^2$ where $p(t,d) = g(t)$ and $p(t,c) = \varepsilon\begin{pmatrix}cos(t)\\sin(t) \end{pmatrix}  +  \mathbf{\mu}, \; \varepsilon \rightarrow 0$ and $\mathbf{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^2$

Fig 2

  Let $$L(s) = \int_{a}^{b}F(p(t,s))\cdot \dot{p}(t,s)\;dt\;\;\;\;(2)$$
So by the fundamental theorem of calculus (see other article)$f(b) = f(a) + \int_{a}^{b}f'(x)\;dx$ we get:
$$L(d) = L(c)+ \int_{c}^{d} \frac{\partial L}{\partial s} \;ds\;\;\;\;(3)$$

Note that $L(d)$ is what we want to compute from the first place.Now substituting (2) on (3)
$$L(d) = L(c) + \int_{c}^{d} \frac{\partial }{\partial s}\left( \int_{a}^{b}F(p(t,s))\cdot \dot{p}(t,s)\;dt\right) \;ds$$
$$\Leftrightarrow L(d) = L(c) + \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}\frac{\partial }{\partial s}(F(p(t,s))\cdot \dot{p}(t,s))\;dt \;ds\;\;\;\;(4)$$
It is simple to show that $$\frac{\partial}{\partial s}(F(p)\cdot \dot p) = \frac{\partial F}{\partial s}\cdot \dot p + F \cdot\frac{\partial \dot p}{\partial s}$$
$$\Leftrightarrow(\mathbf{J}_F \,p_s) \cdot \dot p + F\cdot p_{st} \;\;\;\;(5)$$
Where $\mathbf{J}_F = \begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\\\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}
 & \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}
\end{bmatrix}$ and $p_{st} = \frac{\partial p}{\partial s \partial t}$. Substitute (5) on (4):
$$ L(d) = L(c) + \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}(\mathbf{J}_F \,p_s) \cdot \dot p + F\cdot p_{st}\;dt \;ds\;\;\;\;(6)$$

Its is easy to show that if $\mathbf{u}(x),\mathbf{v}(x):\mathbb{R}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^n$ then :
 $$\frac{d(\mathbf{u}\cdot \mathbf{v})}{dx} = \frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dx}\cdot \mathbf{v}+\mathbf{u}\cdot \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dx}$$
$$\Leftrightarrow \int_{a}^{b} \frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dx}\cdot \mathbf{v} = \left[\mathbf{u}\cdot \mathbf{v}\right]_{a}^{b}-\int_{a}^{b}\mathbf{u}\cdot \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dx}\;\;\;\;(7)$$

With (6) and (7) we get $$(6)\;\;\;\; L(d) = L(c) + \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}(\mathbf{J}_F \,p_s) \cdot \dot p \;dt \;ds + \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}F\cdot p_{st}\;dt \;ds $$

$$\underset{using\;(7)}{\operatorname{\Leftrightarrow}} L(d) = L(c) + \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}(\mathbf{J}_F \,p_s) \cdot p_t \;dt \;ds + \int_{c}^{d} \left(  \left[ F\cdot p_s\right]_{a}^{b} - \int_{a}^{b}(\mathbf{J}_F \,p_t)\cdot p_s \;dt\right )\;ds$$

$$\Leftrightarrow  L(d) = L(c) + \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}(\mathbf{J}_F \,p_s) \cdot p_t - p_s \cdot (\mathbf{J}_F \,p_t) \;dt \;ds + \int_{c}^{d} \left[ F\cdot p_s\right]_{a}^{b}\;ds\;\;\;\;(8)$$

  Now we need to simplify $(\mathbf{J}_F \,p_s) \cdot p_t - p_s \cdot (\mathbf{J}_F \,p_t)$ which is equal to $\left<\mathbf{J}_F \,p_s, p_t\right> - \left<p_s  ,\mathbf{J}_F \,p_t\right> \;\;\;\;(9)$

  To simplify (9) I will expand the more general $\left<\mathbf{M} \,\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\right> - \left <\mathbf{u}  ,\mathbf{M} \,\mathbf{v}\right>$, where $\mathbf{M}\in \mathbb{R}^{2\times 2}$ a two by two matrix.

$$\left <\mathbf{M} \,\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\right> - \left<\mathbf{u}  ,\mathbf{M} \,\mathbf{v}\right> = \sum_{i=1}^{2}\sum_{j=1}^{2}m_{ij}u_jv_i - \sum_{i=1}^{2}\sum_{j=1}^{2}m_{ij}u_iv_j$$ $$\Leftrightarrow \left<\mathbf{M} \,\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\right> - \left<\mathbf{u}  ,\mathbf{M} \,\mathbf{v}\right> =(m_{21}-m_{12})(u_1v_2 - u_2v_1)$$ $$\Leftrightarrow \left<\mathbf{M} \,\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\right> - \left<\mathbf{u}  ,\mathbf{M} \,\mathbf{v}\right> =(m_{21}-m_{12}) |\begin{vmatrix}
 u_1& u_2\\
 v_1&v_2
\end{vmatrix}\;\;\;\;(10)$$

  Note $ \begin{vmatrix}u_1 & u_2\\ v_1 & v_2 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix}u_1 & v_1\\  u_2 & v_2 \end{vmatrix}$

  Using (8), (9) and (10) and that $p(t,s) =\begin{pmatrix}x(t,s)\\ y(t,s)\end{pmatrix}$ we get:
$$L(d) = L(c) + \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}) \begin{vmatrix}
 \frac{\partial x}{\partial s}& \frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\\
 \frac{\partial y}{\partial s}& \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}
\end{vmatrix}\;dt \;ds + \int_{c}^{d} \left[ F\cdot p_s\right]_{a}^{b}\;ds\\
\Leftrightarrow L(d) = L(c) + \int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}) \begin{vmatrix}
 \frac{\partial x}{\partial s}& \frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\\
 \frac{\partial y}{\partial s}& \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}
\end{vmatrix}\;dt \;ds + \int_{c}^{d} <F, p_s(b,s)> - <F,p_s(a,s)>\;ds \;\;\;\; (12)$$
Fig 3

  Now lets try to simplify $$L(c) \;\;\;\;(13)$$ $$\int_{c}^{d} <F, p_s(b,s)> - <F,p_s(a,s)>\;ds\;\;\;\;(14)$$
and $$\int_{c}^{d} \int_{a}^{b}(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}) \begin{vmatrix}
 \frac{\partial x}{\partial s}& \frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\\
 \frac{\partial y}{\partial s}& \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}
\end{vmatrix}\;dt \;ds\;\;\;\;(15)$$

  From (13) we get $$L(c)= \lim_{\varepsilon \rightarrow 0}\int_{a}^{b} \left< F(p(t,c)), \varepsilon\begin{pmatrix}
-\sin(t)\\ \cos(t)\end{pmatrix}\right> dt = 0$$

  We are working with simple closed curves so we can deduce that $p(a,s) = p(b,s), \forall s \in [c,d]$, hence (14) is equal to zero ( check Fig 4).
Fig 4


 From calculus we know that a change of variables has the formula written in Fig 3, therefore (15) $$\iint_D(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}) \begin{vmatrix}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s}& \frac{\partial x}{\partial t}\\\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}& \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\end{vmatrix}\;dt \;ds = \iint_\mathcal{M} (\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y})\ dx\ dy\;\;\;\; (16)$$

Now we can conclude that if $$L(d) = \int_{a}^{b} F(p(t,d)) \cdot p_{t}(t,d)\ dt = \oint_{a}^{b} F(g(t)) \cdot \dot{g}(t) \ dt = \oint_{\partial\mathcal{M}}  P(x,y)\ dx + Q(x,y)\ dy$$
then $$\oint_{\partial\mathcal{M}}  P(x,y)\ dx + Q(x,y)\ dy = \iint_{\mathcal{M}}\left (\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right )\ dx\ dy\;\;\;\; (17)$$

Finally we proof the Green Theorem (17).

Its easy to check by using (17) that $$\oint_{\partial\mathcal{M}}  -Q(x,y)\ dx + P(x,y)\ dy = \iint_{\mathcal{M}}\left (\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} \right )\ dx\ dy$$


We were finally able to compute (1) in a different way. This is a sketch of the Green's theorem proof, where I just wanted to explain the intuition behind Green Theorem, the essential part of this proof was to assume that we have a map $p$ described above. With the same approach we can also try to proof the divergence or Stokes theorem.

This theorem is very useful because we can compute things on the interior of the curve with just the information of the boundary of the curve.

Ex:

We want to compute the area bounded by a simple closed curve(eg fig 3) $$\iint_{\mathcal{M}} 1 \ dx\ dy = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\mathcal{\partial M}} -y \ dx + x \ dy, \;where \; P(x,y)= -\frac{y}{2}, \; Q(x,y) = \frac{x}{2}$$
